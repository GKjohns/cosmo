In the vast emptiness between star systems, traditional navigation methods break down. There are no landmarks, no reliable reference points, and signal delay from known pulsars can introduce drift measured in thousands of kilometers.

![Deep space void](https://picsum.photos/id/120/1200/400){.rounded-lg width="1200" height="400"}

## Three Pillars of Positioning

Modern fleet navigation relies on a layered approach — combining multiple systems to maintain positional accuracy within acceptable margins.

::card-group
  :::card
  ---
  icon: i-lucide-radio-tower
  title: Pulsar Timing Arrays
  ---
  By measuring arrival times of signals from dozens of millisecond pulsars, a vessel can triangulate its position to within a few hundred meters — even at interstellar distances. AEGIS maintains a continuously updated PTA catalog, synchronized across the fleet.
  :::

  :::card
  ---
  icon: i-lucide-orbit
  title: Gravitational Lensing Maps
  ---
  Dark matter concentrations, rogue planets, and residual anomalies from ancient stellar events all distort spacetime in measurable ways. AEGIS integrates gravitational survey data from long-range probes into real-time hazard and reference maps.
  :::

  :::card
  ---
  icon: i-lucide-compass
  title: Inertial Measurement Units
  ---
  When external references go dark, onboard IMUs provide dead-reckoning capability. Modern units drift less than 0.01% per hour — enough to sustain autonomous navigation for days without external correction.
  :::
::

## Navigating in Formation

::picture-and-text
### Fleet Coherence at Speed

Maintaining formation during faster-than-light transit is one of the hardest problems in fleet operations. Even minor timing discrepancies in drive engagement can scatter a formation across light-seconds. AEGIS addresses this with synchronized drive sequencing — a protocol that coordinates ignition timing using quantum-entangled clock signals.

## When You Lose the Signal

What happens when automated systems go dark — from battle damage, solar interference, or hardware failure?

::steps{level="3"}
### Fall back to onboard PTA processor

The ship's local pulsar timing catalog continues to provide positioning autonomously, even without relay contact. Accuracy degrades slowly over days, not hours.

### Switch to inertial dead reckoning

If pulsar signals are also degraded, the IMU takes over. The navigation team begins manual star sights to calibrate drift.

### Manual star sights and computation

The true test of a navigation team. Every navigator must be capable of dead reckoning using basic computational tools. AEGIS logs every instance of manual navigation, building a knowledge base of failure modes that improves fleet-wide resilience.

### Re-acquisition and correction

When automated systems come back online, AEGIS calculates accumulated drift and issues course corrections. The transition back to automated navigation is seamless — the system reconciles manual logs with sensor data automatically.
::

---

### Navigator Training and Certification

Despite the sophistication of automated systems, human navigators remain essential. Their ability to recognize anomalous sensor readings, make judgment calls in degraded conditions, and adapt to situations outside the training data makes them irreplaceable.

AEGIS includes a comprehensive navigator certification program — tracking proficiency scores, simulation hours, and real-world mission performance across every qualified officer in the fleet.
