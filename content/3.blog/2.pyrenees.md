The spatial arrangement of vessels determines sensor coverage, weapons arcs, communications redundancy, and the ability to concentrate or disperse force. A poorly arranged formation creates blind spots that adversaries will exploit.

![Fleet formation overview](https://picsum.photos/id/669/1200/400){.rounded-lg width="1200" height="400"}

## The Five Standard Formations

Modern fleet doctrine recognizes five standard formations, each optimizing for a different operational requirement.

::pictures
![Line formation](https://picsum.photos/id/534/400/300){.rounded-lg height="300" width="400"}
![Wedge formation](https://picsum.photos/id/335/400/300){.rounded-lg height="300" width="400"}
![Sphere formation](https://picsum.photos/id/450/400/300){.rounded-lg height="300" width="400"}
::

**Line** maximizes transit efficiency — vessels follow a single track, minimizing navigational complexity and fuel expenditure. Ideal for long transits through uncontested space.

**Wedge** concentrates offensive capability forward. The lead vessel provides sensor data while flanking vessels maintain overlapping weapons arcs. The standard approach formation for contested environments.

**Sphere** provides all-around defensive coverage. Every approach vector is covered by at least two vessels. Used when the threat axis is unknown or when protecting a high-value asset at center.

::pictures{orientation="vertical"}
![Dispersed screen formation](https://picsum.photos/id/120/800/300){.rounded-lg height="300" width="800"}
![Close escort formation](https://picsum.photos/id/137/800/300){.rounded-lg height="300" width="800"}
::

**Dispersed Screen** maximizes patrol coverage. Vessels spread across a wide area with overlapping sensor zones but minimal weapons overlap. Used for area surveillance and early warning.

**Close Escort** tightly clusters around a protected asset — a supply ship, a command vessel, or a damaged unit withdrawing from engagement. Every escort maintains both sensor coverage and defensive weapons arcs on the protected unit.

## Formation Transitions

::pictures
![Transition phase 1](https://picsum.photos/id/235/300/300){.rounded-lg height="300" width="300"}
![Transition phase 2](https://picsum.photos/id/974/300/300){.rounded-lg height="300" width="300"}
![Transition phase 3](https://picsum.photos/id/550/300/300){.rounded-lg height="300" width="300"}
::

Static formations are a liability in combat. The time required to transition between formations — from Dispersed Screen to Wedge when a threat is identified, or from Wedge to Sphere when engaged from multiple vectors — can determine the outcome of an engagement.

AEGIS monitors formation integrity continuously and can execute automated transitions, calculating optimal vessel movements and timing. During the 2025 Kepler Station joint exercise, AEGIS-equipped squadrons completed formation transitions **40% faster** than manually coordinated units.
