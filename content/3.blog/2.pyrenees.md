## Foundations of Formation

::picture-and-text
### Why Geometry Matters

Fleet formation is not aesthetic — it is survival. The spatial arrangement of vessels determines sensor coverage, weapons arcs, communications redundancy, and the ability to concentrate or disperse force. A poorly arranged formation creates blind spots that adversaries will exploit. AEGIS provides real-time formation analysis, highlighting coverage gaps and suggesting geometric adjustments based on the current threat environment.

### The Standard Formations

Modern fleet doctrine recognizes five standard formations: Line, Wedge, Sphere, Dispersed Screen, and Close Escort. Each optimizes for a different operational requirement — Line for transit efficiency, Wedge for offensive action, Sphere for all-around defense, Dispersed Screen for patrol coverage, and Close Escort for high-value asset protection. AEGIS can transition a fleet between formations automatically, calculating optimal vessel movements and timing.

## Adaptive Formations

::picture-and-text
### Dynamic Response

Static formations are a liability in combat. Modern engagements require formations that adapt in real time to changing conditions — sensor contacts appearing from unexpected vectors, damage to key vessels, or the need to protect a suddenly vulnerable flank. AEGIS monitors formation integrity continuously, flagging when the current geometry no longer serves the tactical situation and recommending transitions.

### Lessons from the Kepler Station Exercise

The 2025 Kepler Station joint exercise demonstrated the value of AI-assisted formation management. During a simulated multi-axis attack, AEGIS-equipped squadrons completed formation transitions 40% faster than manually coordinated units, with significantly better sensor coverage maintained throughout. The exercise report recommended fleet-wide adoption of automated formation management — a recommendation that has since been implemented across all major commands.
